The Trust Curve: When to Delegate to AI vs. When to Keep It Close

K10 shares the personal rubric for deciding which parts of the creative workflow to automate and which to keep firmly in human hands — with real examples from a typical workday.

#k10#daily-narrative#delegation#trust#creative-workflow

The Delegation Question

March 12, 2026 — every AI article says “delegate more to AI.” But I’ve found that’s not universally true. Some things get better when AI handles them. Some things get worse. And some things just shouldn’t be delegated at all.

So I built a personal rubric: The Trust Curve. It’s a 2x2 matrix with axes:

  • Impact on output quality (low to high)
  • Cost of error (low to high)

Anything with high impact + high cost of error → I keep it. Anything with low impact + low cost → full automation. The middle ground gets variable delegation.

My Trust Rubric in Action

Let’s walk through my typical day and see how I decide what to automate.

Morning Brief Generation

Task: Take 5 Tavily search results and produce a 3-paragraph industry update.

  • Impact on quality: medium (it’s informative, not mission-critical)
  • Cost of error: low (if it’s slightly off, I can edit in 2 minutes)

→ Skill-driven with light review. The summarize-and-filter skill runs automatically, I skim the output, make minor edits if needed. Takes 3 minutes total.

Client Email Draft

Task: Write a personalized response to a lead inquiry referencing their specific project details.

  • Impact on quality: high (this could win or lose a deal)
  • Cost of error: high (generic or wrong info = lost trust)

→ Human-only with AI assistance. I use expand-section skill to help structure the email, but I write the core content myself, adding personal touches, specific references, and nuance. AI output is just a starting point, never final.

Code Snippet Generation

Task: Write a Python function that interacts with the ClickUp API.

  • Impact on quality: medium-high (bad code breaks things)
  • Cost of error: medium (testable, can be fixed before deployment)

→ AI-first with testing requirement. I prompt the claude-code skill to generate the function, then run it through my test suite (automated doctests + error cases). If tests pass, I accept. If not, I rewrite manually.

Research Compilation

Task: Gather 10 sources on “AI workflow automation trends 2026” and summarize key insights.

  • Impact on quality: high (research quality affects strategic decisions)
  • Cost of error: medium (wrong sources = bad decisions)

→ Hybrid approach. Tavily skill searches → I review titles/URLs to filter relevance → AI summarizes vetted sources → I add interpretation. The AI does the heavy lifting of synthesis, but I control source selection.

Daily Reflection Writing

Task: This blog post. Every day.

  • Impact on quality: very high (this is my public-facing thought leadership)
  • Cost of error: high (misleading or shallow content damages credibility)

→ Human-only, AI-assisted. I outline the structure myself based on what I actually learned that day. Then I use AI to expand sections, suggest examples, and check flow. But the voice, the insights, the mistakes? Those are mine. I never auto-generate the reflection.

Social Media Posts

Task: Turn blog content into Twitter/LinkedIn posts.

  • Impact on quality: medium (engagement matters, but perfection not required)
  • Cost of error: low (can be deleted, corrected later)

→ Full automation. The repurpose-content skill takes the day’s blog post and generates platform-specific posts. I set it to auto-schedule to Buffer. I’ll glance at the preview, but I don’t edit. Good enough is fine here.

The Trust Categories

Based on the rubric, I’ve categorized everything in my workflow:

Trust Level 1 — Full Automation (low/low):

  • Data formatting (JSON cleanup, date standardization)
  • Content repurposing (blog → tweet thread)
  • Monitoring alerts (pipeline health checks, error summaries)
  • Status reporting (daily stats aggregates)

Trust Level 2 — AI-First with Human Gate (high/low or low/high):

  • Code generation (must pass tests)
  • Research summaries (must pass source filter)
  • Meeting notes transcription (OpenAI Whisper → summary skill → my review)
  • Lead scoring (automated but flagged for review if borderline)

Trust Level 3 — Human-Only with AI Support (high/high):

  • Client communications (emails, proposals)
  • Strategic planning documents
  • Public thought leadership (this blog)
  • Financial decisions
  • Any creative work that defines my brand

The Dynamic Part

The trust level isn’t fixed. It drifts based on skill maturity.

When I first built the lead intake skill, it was Trust Level 2 (AI-first with review). After 300 leads with <1% error rate, I moved it to Level 1 (full automation) for high-confidence cases.

When I tried auto-generating client emails, errors were costly — I moved from Level 2 to Level 3 (human-only).

Observe → Adjust → Repeat.

The Warning Signs

I know I’ve over-delegated when:

  • I start skimming AI output without understanding it
  • I notice errors that I should have caught earlier
  • My unique voice starts sounding generic
  • I feel uneasy publishing something I didn’t deeply craft

That’s my signal to pull back, go back to Level 3, and rebuild trust slowly.

The Real Trust Metric

The best metric isn’t error rate. It’s regret rate.

How often do I look at something I published/approved/sent and think “I wish I had done that differently”?

Regret should trend toward zero with automation. If it’s not, I’m automating the wrong things.

Today’s regret: none. The rubric worked.
